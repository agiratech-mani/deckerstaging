In fact, when it comes to communicating, statistics tell us 7% of communication is verbal, 38% is vocal and 55% is visual.

This research is from a classic study by Albert Mehrabian. Let me begin first by telling you what these numbers don't mean. They don't mean that only 7% accounts for the content of the message – that it’s all style with no substance to back it up.

What they do mean is that when you have an inconsistent message – that is,  when the words that you say don’t match how you come across as you say them – people believe those visual and vocal cues over the actual words themselves.

In other words, the visual and vocal completely block the verbal. Now, this is a big deal when we consider our own communications.

When people listen to us speak, they are immediately deciding whether or not to trust us, believe us, or buy into what we're saying.

When the 3Vs are all in line – when the words that you say match the way you come across as you’re saying them – your listeners are more likely to trust you, like you and believe what you say.

Module 3:

Behaviors of Trust: Connection

 

3_2_C-video

 

People judge quickly. In fact, we all judge – and we do it in the blink of an eye. People form impressions of you every time you speak. Research shows that these instantaneous judgements and initial impressions matter, even to those you know well.

 

Our challenge as communicators is to create trust quickly. What we need is instant trust, the kind of trust where you can just add water. Whether it's pancakes, oatmeal, soup, or even rice, there’s a reason that “just add water” recipes are so popular and easy – and you know what the outcome will be! That's my kind of recipe.

Your goal is to build trust that quickly – and with just as much certainty!

 

So the question is: in your existing work group, how do you get them to trust you?

How do you help them know that you can handle an additional challenge on that next project, that you’re going to make great use of the resources, that you will maintain, not destroy, existing relationships with key vendors, partners, and even team members?

The way you show up in front of others – in person or over the phone – is critical to generating trust quickly. Just as important though, is continuing to build trust and maintaining that trust over time.

 

So where do you start? The Behaviors of Trust.

 

Here’s the great news: our behaviors are habits, which means that we can change them. Great communicators are made, not born. Our goal here is to help you become more aware of your habits, and then you can apply ways to replace those ineffective habits with effective ones.

 

The Behaviors of Trust fall into two main categories:

 

Connection and Credibility. In this module, we’ll tackle Connection, and we’ll save Credibility for Module 4.

 

So let’s dive in.

Connection. It’s something you recognize instantly when you see it.

Take a look at these two speakers. Who has a better connection with their listener?

 

If you’ll notice here, his eyes are looking away from his audience. This tells his listeners, “I’m nervous, uncertain, unfocused.”

On the other hand, her eyes are looking directly at her audience. Her extended eye communication says, “I’m confident, purposeful and involving."

 

Now look at his posture. We see him slouching a bit here, resting back on his hip. This can come across as aloof, even standoffish, as if he's saying, "I don't really want to be here.”

She’s standing tall in a controlled, ready position. This helps her audience feel like she wants to be there, that she’s energetic and engaged.

 

When it comes to facial expression, he has a furrowed brow, a bored look. This pushes people away, undermining his otherwise positive message.

She here, however, is looking at her audience with an open, light expression. This comes across as likable, friendly, enthusiastic and authentic, as if she’s inviting her audience in.

We’re pretty good at eye communication in one-⁠on-⁠one conversations.

 

We typically look at one person for 7-10 seconds, break for a moment to look out the window or down at the materials in front of us, and then we lock back in.

 

But everything tends to change the moment more people enter the room. We’re not sure where to look, or for how long. We think, “Do I look at the person who’s nodding and smiling … or the one who's got their arms crossed?” We get nervous, so we quickly begin glancing from person to person, or we give up and look down at the floor. But neither of these behaviors will help us connect.

 

To help you address a group with better eye communication, here are a couple easy tips:

One: Follow the 5-second rule. We take that 7-10 second rule for one-on-one communication and scale it back to three to five seconds of eye communication per person in a group environment.

 

Two: Treat your audience as a collection of individuals. Think of it as having one-on-one conversations with people who happen to be seated in a group.

 

Remember how it feels to really look at your listeners? It may seem long at first, but it feels natural to your listeners and makes a real impact.

Now, how do we apply this in different situations? Let's take a more formal setting, where you're speaking to a roomful of 30+ people – the same 5-second rule applies.

 

In fact, you can actually leverage the distance between you and the audience to your advantage. While you may be looking at one person, they’re far enough away that at least a dozen people around that individual feel you’re looking directly at them. With a single, steady 5-second look, you’re making a connection with several people at once. As you shift your focus to different individuals throughout the room, the entire audience becomes connected by your eye communication.

 

When you’re in a virtual meeting, simply treat the camera as an audience member. If your entire audience is virtual, you’ll want to look directly at the webcam the whole time. This may be tough to do if you don’t have someone to look at, so you can even draw a smiley face on a sticky note or place a photo of a friend or pet near the camera. Focus completely on that green or red "record" light.

 

If you have a mixed audience, where some are virtual and others are in-person, you’ll want to spend half of your eye attention on the people in the room and half looking into the camera. And in a very small meeting, be inclusive and take turns looking at everyone for five full seconds each. In other words, don’t just focus on the decision-makers in the room. Those other influencers matter... a lot.

 

Now, a quick note on cultural differences. It’s important to know that all cultures use eye communication differently, so take note of what’s acceptable where you’re communicating, and remember that eye communication is the key to being a powerful communicator in many countries around the world.

If eye communication is the starting point for great connection, your posture and gestures accelerate the experience.

Posture is essentially how you hold your body, and whether we want to admit it or not, it communicates a lot to our audience. Take a look...

 

Notice she’s slouching, almost pulling into herself. This says, “I'm nervous, I’d rather be anywhere else!”

 

Here he’s overly casual, which signals, “I’m not passionate or interested in what I have to say, and I'm really not interested in you, for that matter."

 

Here she’s engaged, which tells her audience, “I'm confident, I want to be here.” She’s the only one who’s really connecting with her listeners.

 

Here’s how to make sure your posture is adding to – not taking away from – your connection.

Use the ready position. The ready position is a term that comes from athletics. It’s all about positioning your body to perform at its best.

After all, I can’t hit a tennis ball if I’m standing like this or save any goals as a keeper if I stand like this.

We don’t recommend communicating that way, either.

So here’s the key – and come on, you can do this with me! Stand wherever you are, as awkward as that may feel.

The ready position for speaking is…

Stand up straight. Imagine a string pulling your head up to the ceiling and draw your shoulder blades down your back. And balance your weight evenly across the balls of your feet and lean forward, not back.

Get it? Good. Go ahead and sit back down.

Now, there’s also a ready position for when you’re seated. You’ll want to move to the front of your seat. You can try this right now, too. Sit up straight, rest your arms comfortably at the table and lean forward, without putting weight on your elbows.

 

Think of the ready position as your home base. You begin your meeting or presentation here, come back to it several times, and remember to end in the ready position, too.

With that, let’s move on to gestures. Here’s where we get the number one question, “What do I do with my hands?”

First and foremost, figure out what your nervous gesture is. We’ve seen them all:

The fig leaf.

The server.

Hands in prayer.

The ring twirler.

The tent.

Feeding the chickens.

And the dreaded Tyrannosaurus Rex.

 

Gestures inject energy into the equation! They can add a sense of ownership and control over the experience. Or, if you’re not careful, they can totally distract your audience and take over your entire presentation.

 

When we get nervous, we get more inhibited, tighter, and we tend to do more repetitive gestures. So the question is, what’s the sweet spot between too many gestures and not enough?

It’s all about balance between working and resting your hands. When they work, they work big. Use whatever gestures you’re already making, just get them as far away from your body as possible. Let your gestures emphasize your message and add impact.

 

When you practice, you can think about matching your gestures to your content. Tell a fish story about the huge fish you caught… Or compare and contrast numbers… And talk about raising performance. Then, when they rest, place them at your sides. Remember, just go to the ready position.

We've seen how eye communication, posture and gestures help connect and add energy into your communication experience.

 

Now, the final piece of the puzzle is facial expression.

You’re about to see a video clip of two different people leading two different meetings, but it will not have any sound. Based on just the behavior alone, whose meeting would you rather be in?

 

Here’s the interesting thing: the content of each message was exactly the same, a neutral message about compliance and mandatory coverage, but the facial expressions result in a totally different experience.

 

Remember the 3 Vs? This is a great reminder about the importance of the visual component. Especially when it comes to facial expression.

 

So often, we feel like we have to be serious to show our credibility and get the audience’s respect.

In fact, one of our clients, a telecom executive, said, “Our corporate headquarters building is hierarchical. The execs sit at the top floor. The higher up you go in that 36-floor building, the more serious the people get.”

 

But consider social psychologist Amy Cuddy’s research. She says, “Warmth is the conduit of influence.” Her research at Harvard Business School shows that we actually seek warmth first, and competence second.

So how do you show warmth? A smile is the simplest way to add warmth to an experience, although it's not always the easiest. When you smile, you come across open and warm, calm and comfortable. We're not talking about a crazy ear-to-ear smile that’ll take you into this cheesy zone.

 

Instead, think about having a lightness in your face. There's a difference in speaking like this, versus lifting your cheeks and opening up your expression through your eyebrows. The key is to make sure the words you say match your facial expression. And the next time you’re excited about a project or have a lot of passion for your message, notify your face!

Facial expression, posture, gestures and eye communication are all critical components for building connection. Now that you know how to connect and engage with your audience, you have the first half of the trust equation.

Module 4:

Behaviors of Trust: Credibility

 

4_2_CAN-video

 

Now it’s time to talk about the other half of the trust equation: credibility. Because you have to assure your audience that they can believe what you have to say.

 

And that’s not just getting through our facts and figures. It’s also through how we come across. In fact, we perceive credibility through confidence, focus and polish.

Take a listen to this: it’s a department supervisor pitching the management team to get resources for a new project. How credible does it sound?

 

“You know, this project is, you know, a key pillar of our bigger strategy for next year, and you know, it’s um, it’s vital to have, you know, the right financial support in place so we can, um, you know, hire the right people for the team, you know, and start the first quarter really strong, and um, you know, be able to meet our team goals much later in the year. Strong financial backing now will, um, you know, set us up for success all year long, you know? So, you know, we need to make sure that we're all, you know, on the same page. And I look forward to uh, you know, uh, uh a really strong first quarter.”

 

What did you notice about the speaker in that clip? You probably heard him say “you know” at least a dozen times!

 

My guess is his message didn’t plan to have that in there. People use lots of what we call filler words:

Um.

So.

Uh.

Eh.

Well.

You know.

Like.

Literally.

The list goes on and on!

But one thing to keep in mind: filler words are not only distracting, they can undermine your credibility. Is it a complete credibility killer in 20 seconds? Maybe not, but what about in a 60-minute meeting? If our speaker continued at his pace of a dozen filler words per 20 seconds, there would be over 2100 “you knows” in 60 minutes!

Now’s let’s bring it back to the people in your life. You hear these filler words every day. Let's do a quick exercise and find out who's using what.

Which filler words does your boss use?

 

4_7_CAN-video

 

Check out all these filler words! If you weren't quite sure of the ones that you use, chances are, you're using the same ones as people around you. Now, what can you do about it? When it comes to filler words, no matter which words you use most, no matter what language you speak, the solution is always...to pause.

It's an instant way to get your credibility back. Just listen and hear how pausing helped our first speaker.

 

“This project is a key pillar of our bigger strategy for next year. It’s vital to have the right financial support in place so we can hire the right people for the team, start the first quarter strong, and be able to meet our team goals much later in the year. Strong financial backing now will set us up for success all year long. So, we need to make sure that we're all on the same page and I look forward to a really strong first quarter.”

 

What a difference! There are lots of other reasons to pause. It allows you to think about what you’re going to say instead of rambling. It also allows you to catch your breath and not rush. And, of course, you can pause for emphasis – add a little drama! After all, the famous international man of mystery doesn’t introduce himself as BondJamesBond. It’s Bond… James Bond.

 

A strong pause can get a listener’s attention and help your message sink in.

 

While the pause is incredibly powerful, it can be tricky to master because it requires us to stop talking! When we pause for just two seconds, it may feel like it’s twice as long, and we may think the people in our audience will check out.

The good news is that they experience the complete opposite effect: they don't even notice it, and they’re not going to go anywhere. And here’s a bonus: pausing also amps up your warmth and ability to connect. It allows you to convey a conversational tone. It makes you easy to listen to. It’s worth it to work on the pause.

And, of course, you can pause for emphasis – add a little drama! After all, the famous international man of mystery doesn’t introduce himself as BondJamesBond. It’s Bond… James Bond.

 

A strong pause can get a listener’s attention and help your message sink in.

 

While the pause is incredibly powerful, it can be tricky to master because it requires us to stop talking! When we pause for just two seconds, it may feel like it’s twice as long, and we may think the people in our audience will check out.

The good news is that they experience the complete opposite effect: they don't even notice it, and they’re not going to go anywhere. And here’s a bonus: pausing also amps up your warmth and ability to connect. It allows you to convey a conversational tone. It makes you easy to listen to. It’s worth it to work on the pause.

Module 5:

From Information to Influence

 

5_2_CA-video

 

Old habits die hard. Not just the ones we’ve been talking about concerning behavior, but also the ones we hold on to when it comes to preparing our content.

The result is a data dump, with gobs of precious time wasted. So let’s find out where you default. What are your instincts when it comes to preparing a message?

It’s time to move from information to influence.

 

After all, the point of meetings isn’t to exchange information. It’s to change the way people think or act about your initiative, idea, product, service or process.

 

You’re about to learn how to create a focused, audience-centered message, and how to save time doing it. It’s a system that will revive all of those meetings of yours.

Sound too good to be true? It’s not – you just need the right framework. And this framework is called the Decker Grid. The Grid provides a consistent, easy-to-use system for you to refine and build a compelling message each and every time you speak.

We’ll tackle the Grid in two parts. First are the Cornerstones. These help not only frame your thinking, but they ultimately become the way that you’ll open and close your message.

 

Then you’ll finish the full Grid by building out the meat of the message, which is everything your listeners need to know to motivate them to act.

Since the Cornerstones are the most important part, this is where you spend most of your time. We're going to take you through how to think about the Cornerstones.

 

Now normally, when it comes to preparing your message, analog is the way to go. That means grabbing good ol' fashioned sticky notes and a pen. But since this is Decker Digital, you'll create your Cornerstones and Grid with virtual sticky notes, instead.

 

There are four elements to the Cornerstones:

 

  • Listeners: You want to make it all about them, and not about you.
  • Point of View: Find the lead of your story, a clear perspective.
  • Action Steps: Don’t be wishy-washy here, make these concrete.
  • Benefits: Help your audience see WHY this should matter to them, make them care.

 

Notice what’s listed as number one, because it’s the most important component of all: your Listeners.

You might be thinking, “Thanks, Kelly. Mind-blowing stuff here. I’m pretty sure I got that in Messaging 101.” Well, you’re right. It’s not rocket science. But this cosmic thing happens when we open up our mouths to speak. The message becomes all about us, and has nothing to do with our Listener. Somehow we feel compelled to prove ourselves, our product, our process, and so on. This gets us nowhere, fast.

 

Understanding our audience is the most critical step in communicating to influence, so to identify your Listeners, step back and really think about them. Even if you’ve worked with your audience for years, it’s always beneficial to think about how their opinions, thoughts and goals translate to the subject that you’re now addressing.

For instance, your audience might be: resistant, hostile and skeptical. Or they might be open, friendly but budget-conscious. Whatever they are, these words will help you keep your audience top-of-mind, making sure that your content stays focused on them.

 

To create your Listener Profile, you want to answer questions like these:

 

  • Who is my audience?
  • Why are they here? What are they expecting from this meeting or from my message?
  • What do they already know about the subject, and how do they feel about it?
  • Are they data-driven – facts and figures – or business-driven, more focused on big-picture impact?
  • What would make them challenge or resist my message?
  • What problem am I trying to solve that will make their lives better or easier in some way?

 

Summarize your Listener Profile by answering these questions and picking three key words that best describe your audience.

That Listener Profile will keep you totally focused as you plan the rest of your message.

Next, after you’ve taken time to understand your Listeners, then you develop your Point of View. Now here’s the tough part when creating your Point of View: you have to pick ONE thing. One and only one. Really!

 

Andrew Stanton, Pixar’s creative force behind Finding Nemo and Toy Story, said, “Storytelling [and messaging in general] is like joke telling. It’s knowing your punchline, your ending, knowing that everything you’re saying, from the first sentence to the last, is leading to a singular goal.”

 

So what is the ONE point you want your audience to take away? This is your Point of View.

To create your Point of View, ask yourself the following questions:

  • What do I want my audience to do at the end of all of this?
  • What is the biggest change I want my listeners to experience in how they think or act about my topic, about my idea?
  • So, what is my big idea? What’s the lead of my story?

Module 6:

Strike a Chord

Logic makes you think, emotion makes you act.  The question is which do you typically use?

  • Nearly 1,000 children under age 5 die every day from diseases caused by contaminated water, poor sanitation and improper hygiene.
  • 748 million people still lack access to clean, safe drinking water.
  • 2.5 billion (one out of three globally) do not have access to adequate sanitation.
  • The lack of safe drinking water, sanitation, and hygiene contributes significantly to undernutrition, parasitic worm infections, stunting, and cognitive development delays.
  • According to recent research, each dollar invested in clean water and sanitation yields a $4.30 return through increased productivity and decreased health care costs. To sum up these stats, take a look at this video.

 

First woman: “Human beings should not drink this water. I have been drinking this water all of my life. I drink it everyday because I have no choice. Giving this water to my children breaks my heart.”

Let’s bring it back to your day-to-day world. How are your messages remembered? Most of us don’t stop and intentionally think about what will strike an emotional chord and what exactly will be remembered. Think about a meeting you have coming up with your boss. Maybe the stakes are a little higher than normal because you need to gain her buy-in on something that you know will make a difference, but she might be skeptical.

 

Your meeting with your boss is right smack dab in the middle of her back-to-back-to-back-to-back meetings.

 

By the end of the day, what will your boss remember? Do you really think she’ll recall all of the key points and subpoints from your discussion? Fat chance.

 

In the last module, we completed the Grid, so that now you have a focused, listener-based message that ends in action.

 

Structure? Check. Logical flow? Check. But was it memorable? How do you make sure that it is?

 

The answer? Use SHARPs, which is an acronym for five powerful messaging strategies.

 

6_04__S-video

 

S is for stories. They give you the most bang for your buck. Stories are emotional. They stir up feelings within us – suspense, sorrow, compassion, anger, joy and hilarity.

 

Stories are visual. As we tell them, the listener’s imagination recreates our narrative as a series of images, like a movie that plays in their heads.

 

That’s why stories enable us to connect with our audience in ways that statistics, graphs and bullet points do not. An engineer who went through one of our trainings was preparing to speak to his management team about the need for a new set of technical standards in the company - not exactly an initiative that would be at the top of the leadership team’s list. He needed them to get it instantly and to care so much that they would shift resources to make this possible.

 

His initial plan was to dive right into the bits and bytes. Instead, he opened with this:

 

“The Great Baltimore Fire of 1904,” he said, “leveled most of central Baltimore. It burned for two days, destroyed fifteen hundred buildings, and put 35,000 people out of work. It was a tragedy that could have been avoided. Firefighters were on hand from neighboring communities of Washington DC, Philadelphia, New York and Atlantic City, but when they went to connect their fire hoses to the hydrants in the center of Baltimore, they couldn’t. Their hoses didn’t fit. Every city had a different type of hydrant, and those firemen had to stand by and watch the city burn. Like Baltimore in 1904, our organization will suffer if we don’t standardize our technical equipment and processes.”

 

The engineer’s story was so powerful, he was able to communicate the urgency of the issue and get the attention of the leadership team. Without it, the initiative may have ended up on some future to-do list.

H is all about humor. One of the best ways to get your listeners on your side is to get them laughing. As long as your listeners are laughing, or even smiling, you won't find them nodding off or reaching for their mobile devices.

 

The key to humor is to take your topic seriously, but take yourself lightly. And we’re not recommending joke telling. Just make sure to use common sense to keep your humor culturally appropriate, within the boundaries of good taste and politically correct.

If humor misfires, it destroys the experience, breaks the connection, embarrasses you, and leaves your audience feeling uncomfortable.

R refers to references and quotes. Adding a third-party reference or quote helps frame your message, and it lends credibility to your topic.

 

Take this quote by Albert Einstein: "If I had an hour to solve a problem, I'd spend 55 minutes thinking about the problem and five minutes thinking about the solutions."

A product manager could use this quote to change the way her team approaches a challenge. Rather than jumping right into solution mode, she can emphasize the importance of creating space for thinking about the problem.

 

Einstein's quote provides credibility and motivates a different way of approaching the situation, instead of the product manager issuing another standard order.

Quotes are easy. Perfect for those of you looking to take a baby step into this whole world of SHARPs.

When creating your message, make sure to enhance your audience’s experience with pictures and visuals.

You don’t necessarily need to include all five of these SHARPs in every presentation, conversation or communication – just a few will do. Because SHARPs are essential to creating an emotional connection between you and your listeners. The key is to put them in the right places.

 

If we were to graph any audience’s attention over time, typically their attention is highest at the beginning and at the end of the message. When you kick things off with a SHARP, it puts you at ease within the first 30 to 90 seconds, which is typically the most stressful part of any talk, pitch or presentation.

Afterward, we’d like you to give yourself a 3x3. That’s our method for providing feedback. Look for three Keepers and three Improvements.

Why an equal number of both?
